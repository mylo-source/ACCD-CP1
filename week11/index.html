Carbonated Drink Simulation

Intent

As a lover of anything fizzy, a system that I wanted to explore was carbonated drinks and their tendency to bubble under pressure, and potentially, explode if shaken too much. I must say that this is a deceptively simple everyday phenomenon, but once broken down, it reveals a whole network of interacting relationships: internal pressure, bubble formation, fluid dynamics, the strength of the bottle cap, and the cascading outcome that occurs once pressure exceeds a certain threshold.

Process

I began by constructing a UML diagram and a flowchart to map out my thinking into applicable logic for code. This proved to be extremely useful for mapping out the system in its entirety before jumping into the deep end. These diagrams helped me determine the key objects needed for the simulation, their properties and behaviors, and how they would interact with one another.

In my system, there are four primary objects:

  • Bottle
  • Cap
  • Bubbles within the liquid
  • WaterSplash that emerges when the cap bursts

Because this concept was more complex than what we covered in class, I did use artificial intelligence to help structure and debug the code. Together, we iterated on each class so that the relationships between objects felt coherent, reactive and mirrored what I had mapped out in my diagrams.

To simulate pressure and make it user-controlled, I made the bottle interactive: the user must click repeatedly and quickly to build pressure high enough for the cap to burst. I also added additional forms of indirect control: a dropdown to change the drink type (sparkling water, Coke, Fanta, lemonade), which alters the liquid’s color, and a tilt slider that rotates the bottle and affects the direction of the splash when the cap pops.

An essential component for making this concept come to life was feedback. Users needed to understand how close they were to popping the cap, so I implemented a pressure bar that fills as they click. This provides clarity for the user in terms of actions they must take moving forward depending on if they want the cap to pop off.

Looking back, the entire process was highly iterative. As I continued working, new ideas emerged regarding how to make the simulation more dynamic, refined, and realistic. For example, at first the splash appeared when the cap popped, but the liquid inside the bottle remained constant. To make it resemble what we would see in real life, I implemented a liquid-level system so the fluid visibly drains as it sprays out. This is just one of many revisions that evolved the project from a simple prototype into a more realistic and expressive system.

Overall, this has been one of the most challenging projects I’ve completed in this class. At the same time, however, it has also been one of the most fun and creatively rewarding. I found that the complexity of the system I set out to create opened up a lot of room for experimentation, playfulness, and exploration. I’m very proud of how interactive, responsive, and engaging the final concept became!
